Neural Pathways

Term: What are the main neural pathways involved in motor control?

Definition: Key pathways include the corticospinal tract (descending pathway from brain to spinal cord), the pyramidal tract (portion of corticospinal tract), and pathways involving the cerebellum (e.g., spinocerebellar and cerebellospinal tracts).

Motor Unit

Term: What is a motor unit?

Definition: A motor unit consists of a motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it innervates.

Motor Unit - Innervation Ratio

Term: What is the innervation ratio of a motor unit?

Definition: The innervation ratio is the number of muscle fibers per motor neuron. Finer motor control is associated with smaller innervation ratios, while power generation is associated with larger innervation ratios.

Motor Unit - Force Generation

Term: How do motor units increase force generation?

Definition: Motor units increase force via rate coding (increased impulse frequency) and recruitment (activating more motor units).

Motor Unit - Recruitment

Term: What is recruitment in the context of motor units?

Definition: Recruitment is the activation of additional motor units to increase muscle force. Motor units can fatigue.

Henneman’s Size Principle

Term: What is Henneman’s size principle?

Definition: Smaller, less fatigable muscle fibers are recruited first.

Neuromuscular Junction

Term: What is the role of the neuromuscular junction?

Definition: It’s the site where the nervous system communicates with the muscular system to produce movement.

Synaptic Transmission

Term: What is synaptic transmission?

Definition: The process by which neurons communicate with each other, involving excitatory (depolarizing) and inhibitory (hyperpolarizing) effects.

Action Potential

Term: What is the role of sodium and potassium ions in an action potential?

Definition: An action potential involves the influx of sodium (Na+) ions to depolarize the neuron and the efflux of potassium (K+) ions to repolarize it.

Cerebellum - Function

Term: What are the primary functions of the cerebellum?

Definition: The cerebellum is crucial for coordination, balance, speech, and learning/adapting movements to changing contexts. It adjusts the force, timing, and accuracy of movements.

Cerebellar Ataxia

Term: What is cerebellar ataxia?

Definition: Damage to the cerebellum resulting in loss of coordination.

Cerebellum - Muscle Tone

Term: How does the cerebellum affect muscle tone?

Definition: Cerebellar damage can cause hypertonia (abnormally high muscle tone) or hypotonia (abnormally low muscle tone), depending on the species.

Basal Ganglia - Function

Term: What are the main functions of the basal ganglia?

Definition: The basal ganglia are involved in action selection, regulating voluntary motor movements, procedural learning, routine behaviors, eye movements, cognition, and emotion. They regulate the initiation and smooth execution of movements.

Parkinson’s Disease - Basal Ganglia

Term: How is Parkinson’s disease related to the basal ganglia?

Definition: Parkinson’s disease involves decreased functioning of the substantia nigra within the basal ganglia, leading to a decrease in dopamine and impaired movement.

Motor Cortex - Function

Term: What is the primary function of the motor cortex?

Definition: The motor cortex is responsible for voluntary control of movement, including force and coordination of muscle action. It is spatially mapped to different body regions.

Motor Cortex - Hemispheres

Term: How do the brain’s hemispheres control the body?

Definition: One hemisphere of the motor cortex controls the opposite side of the body.

Stroke - Motor Cortex

Term: How does a stroke affect motor function?

Definition: A stroke, or lack of blood flow to the brain, can affect the motor cortex, often resulting in hemiparesis (one-sided paralysis).

Premotor Cortex - Function

Term: What is the role of the premotor cortex (PMC)?

Definition: The premotor cortex prepares a person for movement in response to stimuli. It has a direct connection with the visual cortex and primarily innervates the trunk and shoulders.

Supplementary Motor Area - Function

Term: What is the function of the Supplementary Motor Area (SMA)?

Definition: The SMA is responsible for the planning and initiation of movement (internal cueing).

Action Potential at Motor End Plate

Term: What occurs when an action potential reaches the motor end plate?

Definition: Voltage-gated Ca2+ channels open, ACh is released into the synaptic cleft, ACh binds to receptors on the muscle fiber, generating an action potential and initiating excitation-contraction coupling.

Neurotransmitters - Acetylcholine (ACh)

Term: What is the function of Acetylcholine (ACh)?

Definition: Enables muscle action, learning, and memory. Deterioration of ACh-producing neurons is associated with Alzheimer’s disease.

Neurotransmitters - Dopamine

Term: What is the function of Dopamine?

Definition: Influences movement, learning, attention, and emotion. Imbalances are linked to schizophrenia (oversupply) and Parkinson’s disease (undersupply).

Practical Implications - Injury Prevention

Term: How does understanding motor control pathways aid in injury prevention?

Definition: It assists in designing effective injury prevention programs and rehabilitation protocols for athletes and patients.

Practical Implications - Athletic Performance

Term: How can knowledge of motor loops enhance athletic performance?

Definition: It can be applied to optimize training regimens, improving coordination, timing, and precision.

Practical Implications - Movement Analysis

Term: Why is proficiency in motor control important for movement analysis?

Definition: It allows for sophisticated movement analysis, crucial for identifying performance bottlenecks and correcting techniques.

Practical Implications - Neuroplasticity

Term: How does understanding neuroplasticity impact training after an injury?

Definition: It can inform techniques to retrain the nervous system, leading to more effective recovery strategies.

Practical Implications - Motor Learning

Term: Why is grasping motor control concepts fundamental for teaching motor skills?

Definition: It is fundamental for teaching and refining motor skills across various populations.

Practical Implications - Technology

Term: How can technology be utilized to study motor control in kinesiology?

Definition: Utilizing motion capture and other technologies can lead to breakthroughs in virtual reality and augmented feedback for training and therapy.

Practical Implications - Interdisciplinary Approach

Term: Why is an interdisciplinary approach important in studying human movement and health?

Definition: Integrating motor control knowledge with biomechanics, physiology, and psychology leads to a holistic approach.

Motor and Sensory Loop

Term: How do motor and sensory loops ensure movements are precise?

Definition: Continuous feedback loops between motor and sensory areas ensure that movements are adjusted based on real-time sensory information.
